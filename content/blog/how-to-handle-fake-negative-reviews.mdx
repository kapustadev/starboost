Every business owner dreads the notification: a new 1-star review. Whether it's from a genuinely unhappy customer, an internet troll, or a malicious competitor trying to ruin your online reputation, the damage to your brand is immediate.

So, what is the first thing most business owners try to do? They try to delete it.

## The Myth of Deleting Bad Reviews

Platforms like Google Maps, Facebook, and Trustpilot heavily guard their review ecosystems. They almost never delete a review simply because a business owner flags it as "fake" or "unfair." Unless the review contains explicit hate speech, doxxing, or blatant spam links, the platform's support team will likely respond with an automated email stating that the review "does not violate our guidelines."

Fighting with support for months while your 1-star review sits at the top of your profile actively driving away customers is a losing battle.

## The Solution: Dilution

If you cannot delete a negative review, the only effective strategy is to **dilute** it. 

Consider the math: If you have five reviews and one is a 1-star, your average plummets to a disastrous 3.4. But if you have fifty 5-star reviews and one 1-star review, your average is a pristine 4.9. The negative review becomes a mere blip on the radar, often buried deep beneath pages of glowing praise.
